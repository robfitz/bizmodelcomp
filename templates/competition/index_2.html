Case study

UCL's EChallenge had 650 applicants to their 4-phase competition, supplemented by weekly workshop events. Ultimately, 30 finalists gave live presentations, which were evaluated in combination with their online submissions to select winners across 4 categories for prizes totaling €20,000.

Nvana was used throughout the event for application management and judging. Its use saved significant amounts of organizer time related to judging and allowed teams to receive their judge feedback immediately.

Since then, UCL has run two additional events using Nvana, giving out nearly €100,000 to student entrepreneurs through the platform.

Back to topPhilosophy

Avoid the dollhouse fallacy

Startups are not miniature versions of big companies. As such, the business advice and strategic tools developed in a corporate context are frequently irrelevant and can even be damaging to young startups. The most important activities for a founder are learning and iteration, not planning and execution.

Embrace extreme uncertainty

Startups are solving unknown problems for unknown customers by building an unknown solution. Under such conditions, asking founders to plan years ahead is an exercise in futility. Instead of disguising guesses as budgets and roadmaps, founders should be taught to reduce their business risks through an iterative, experimental approach.

Prioritise deep customer understanding

The fastest, cheapest, and most effective way to improve a startup's odds of success is for its founders to constantly be talking to potential customers. Instead of treating is as a sidenote, gathering customer feedback is considered a crucial deliverable and disproving your core beliefs is celebrated as progress.

Stop wasting student time

The four most difficult parts of creating a startup are 1) forming a team, 2) discovering a worthwhile problem to solve, 3) articulating a value proposition, and 4) finding your first customer. Roughly 3½ of those can (and should!) be tackled by students. Instead of teaching them to polish an imperfect plan, let's see them start their business.
